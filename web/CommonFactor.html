    Section 2.5 Factoring: Common Factor

    What is a factor?
    🔗
    When it comes to multiplying, a factor is one of the things being multiplied and the product is the result that is obtained from multiplying. For example in the equation \(2 \cdot 6 = 12\text{,}\) \(2\) and \(6\) are factors and the product is \(12\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Recall from section 2.2; the distributive law gives us \(a(b+c)=ab+ac\text{.}\) Here \(a\) and \(b+c\) are the factors and the product is \(ab+ac\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Putting this into a context, we have \(3(x+5)=3x+15\) with factors \(3\) and \((x+5)\) and a product of \(3x+15\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Subsection 2.5.1 Common Factor

    We can use a common factor to reverse the distributive law we were introduced to in section 2.2. A common factor is a term that divides each term of the expression. We are looking for our result to be factored completely , meaning that there are no other factors that can be divided out. When looking at the example above, \(3\) is a common factor of the expression \(3x+15\) because \(3\) divides both \(3x\) and \(15\) evenly.
    🔗

    Example 2.5.1.

    Factor the expression \(10x^2+25x\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We are looking for a common factor that divides both \(10x^2\) and \(25x\text{.}\) Beginning with the coefficients, \(5\) divides both \(10\) and \(25\) and the highest power of \(x\) that divides both \(x^2\) and \(x\) is \(x\text{.}\) Therefore our common factor of \(10x^2\) and \(25x\) is \(5x\text{.}\) When we divide both terms in our original expression by \(5x\text{,}\) we have:
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{10x^2}{5x} + \frac{25x}{5x} = \frac{\cancel{10}^{~2}\cdot \cancel{x}^{~1} \cdot x}{\cancel{5}^{~1}\cdot\cancel{x}^{~1}} + \frac{\cancel{25}^{~5}\cdot\cancel{x}^{~1}}{\cancel{5}^{~1}\cdot\cancel{x}^{~1}} = \frac{2x}{1} + \frac{5}{1} = 2x+5 \end{equation*}
    and we obtain:
    \begin{equation*} 10x^2+25x=5x(2x+1) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Because the terms \(2x\) and \(5\) share no other common factors (other than 1) we have completely factored our expression. We can use the distributive law to check if our factored expression is correct:
    \begin{equation*} 5x(2x+1)=5x(2x)+5x(5)=10x^2+25x \end{equation*}
    🔗
    When multiplying the factors \(5x\) and \(2x+5\text{,}\) our product was the expression we started with, so we can see we factored the expression appropriately.
    🔗

    Example 2.5.2.

    Factor the expression \(4x^3+20x^2\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We are looking for a common factor that divides both \(4x^3\) and \(20x^2\text{.}\) Beginning with the coefficients, both \(2\) is a common factor of \(4\) and \(20\) and the highest power of \(x\) that divides both \(x^3\) and \(x^2\) is \(x^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We can use the common factor \(2x^2\text{.}\) When we divide both terms in our original expression by \(2x^2\) we have:
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{4x^3}{2x^2} + \frac{20x^2}{2x^2} = \frac{\cancel{4}^{~2} \cdot \cancel{x}^{~1} \cdot\cancel{x}^{~1} \cdot x}{\cancel{2}^{~1}\cdot\cancel{x}^{~1} \cdot\cancel{x}^{~1}} + \frac{\cancel{20}^{~10}\cdot\cancel{x}^{~1} \cdot\cancel{x}^{~1}}{\cancel{2}^{~1}\cdot\cancel{x}^{~1} \cdot\cancel{x}^{~1}} = \frac{2x}{1} + \frac{10}{1} = 2x+10 \end{equation*}
    so the factorization we have obtained is \(4x^3+20x^2=2x^2(2x+10)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    However, the terms inside the parenthesis share another common factor of \(2\text{,}\) so our result is not completely factored. We would then have to factor out another \(2\text{,}\) leaving us with:
    \begin{equation*} 4x^3+20x^2=2x^2(2x+10)=2x^2(2)(x+5)=4x^2(x+5) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Notice that the common factor after we divided out another \(2\text{,}\) becomes \(4x^2\text{.}\) If we had originally divided both terms in our original expression by \(4x^2\text{,}\) we would have \(4x^2(x+5)\text{.}\) }
    🔗
    Although we have the same result in either case, it is more efficient to factor out \(4x^2\) initially as it has a larger coefficient than \(2x^2\text{.}\) We can call \(4x^2\) the greatest common factor (GCF) of \(4x^3\) and \(20x^2\) because that is the factor with the largest coefficient and highest degree literal part that divides both terms.
    🔗

    Example 2.5.3.

    Factor the expression \(3x+21\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Beginning with the coefficients, \(3\) divides both \(3\) and \(21\) and because \(21\) has no literal part, the variable cannot be a part of our common factor. Therefore, our greatest common factor is \(3\text{.}\) When we divide both terms in our original expression by \(3\text{,}\) we have:
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{3x}{3} + \frac{21}{3} = \frac{\cancel{3}^{~1} \cdot x}{\cancel{3}^{~1}} + \frac{\cancel{21}^{~7}}{\cancel{3}^{~1}} = \frac{x}{1} + \frac{7}{1} = x+7 \end{equation*}
    and we obtain:
    \begin{equation*} 3x+21=3(x+7) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Because the terms \(x\) and \(7\) share no other common factors our expression is factored completely. }
    🔗

    Example 2.5.4.

    Factor the expression \(-6x+9\)
    🔗
    🔗
    \(-6\) and \(9\) are both divisible by \(3\) and again, the second term in the expression has no literal part so the variable will not be included in our common factor. When we divide by 3 we have:
    \begin{equation*} -6x+9=3(-2x+3) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We can also consider a common factor of \(-3\) being that our leading term has a negative coefficient. When we divide out a -3 we have:
    \begin{equation*} -6x+9=-3(2x-3) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Both are factored completely and when using the distributive property, both give back the original expression.
    \begin{equation*} 3(-2x+3)=3(-2x)+3(3)=-6x+9 \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} -3(2x-3)=-3(2x)-3(-3)=-6x+9 \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 2.5.5.

    Factor the expression \(-6x^2-18x\)
    🔗
    🔗
    \(6\) divides both \(-6\) and \(-18\) and the highest power of \(x\) that divides both \(x^2\) and \(x\) is \(x\text{.}\) Because both terms in the original expression are negative, we can use \(-6x\) as our common factor. When we divide by \(-6x\) we have:
    \begin{equation*} -6x^2-18x=-6x(x+3) \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 2.5.6.

    Factor the expression \(8a^3b-24ab^2+16a^2b^3\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We are looking for a common factor of \(8a^3b\text{,}\) \(-24ab^2\text{,}\) and \(16a^2b^3\text{.}\) \(8\) divides the coefficients \(8\text{,}\) \(-24\) and \(16\text{.}\) The literal part of all three terms here contain both \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) Therefore, we need to consider both variables when looking for our common factor. The highest power of \(a\) that divides \(a^3\text{,}\) \(a\text{,}\) and \(a^2\) is \(a\) and the highest power of \(b\) that divides \(b\text{,}\) \(b^2\text{,}\) and \(b^3\) is \(b\text{.}\) Therefore our common factor is \(8ab\text{.}\) When we divide out \(8ab\text{,}\) we obtain:
    \begin{equation*} 8a^3b-24ab^2+16a^2b^3=8ab(a^2-3b+2ab^2) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We can of course use the distributive law to check our solution:
    \begin{equation*} 8ab(a^2-3b+2ab^2)=8ab(a^2)+8ab(-3b)+8ab(2ab^2)=8a^3b-24ab^2+16a^2b^3 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Looking back to the beginning of this section, specifically at the expression \(ab+ac\text{,}\) we can see that the two terms share a common factor of \(a\text{.}\) We can then divide \(a\) from each term leaving us with \(a(b+c)\text{.}\) In some cases, it will be appropriate to use an expression with more than one term as our common factor.
    🔗

    Example 2.5.7.

    Factor the expression \(3x^2(x-1)+4(x-1)\)
    🔗
    🔗
    \((x-1)\) is a factor of both \(3x^2(x-1)\) and \(4(x-1)\) using the same pattern as above, we can factor this expression into \((x-1)(3x^2+4)\text{.}\) In both forms we can see that both the \(3x^2\) and \(4\) are being distributed to the factor of \((x-1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.5.2 Factor by Grouping

    If we need to factor an expression with four terms, we can look for a common factor that contains more than one term. This method is known as factoring by grouping.
    🔗

    Example 2.5.8.

    Factor \(x^3-3x^2+2x-6\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Now we are working with an expression with four terms, none of which have a common factor greater than \(1\text{.}\) We can split our expression into two groups of terms first looking at \(x^3-3x^2\) and noticing that this expression has a common factor of \(x^2\) and when we divide out that term, we are left with \(x^2(x-3)\text{.}\) Let’s now look at the second group, \(2x-6\) and notice that this expression has a common factor of \(2\) and when we divide out the \(2\text{,}\) we are left with \(2(x-3)\text{.}\) Notice that we have a common factor now of \(x-3\text{.}\) We can then factor:
    \begin{equation*} x^3-3x^2+2x-6=x^2(x-3)+2(x-3)=(x^2+2)(x-3) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We can of course use the distributive law to check our solution:
    \begin{equation*} (x^2+2)(x-3)=x^2(x)+x^2(-3)+2(x)+2(-3)=x^3-3x^2+2x-6 \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 2.5.9.

    Factor the expression \(ab-6a+2b-12\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Splitting our expression into two groups, let’s look at the first group \(ab-6a\text{.}\) We see that this expression shares a common factor of \(a\text{,}\) and when we divide that term out, we are left with \(a(a-6)\text{.}\) The second group \(2b-12\) shares a common factor of \(2\) and when we divide that term out, we are left with \(2(b-6)\text{.}\) Both groups share a common factor of \(b-6\) therefore we can factor:
    \begin{equation*} ab-6a+2b-12=a(a-6)+2(a-b)=(a+2)(b-6) \end{equation*}
