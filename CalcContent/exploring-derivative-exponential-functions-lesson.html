    Section 2.4 Lesson: Exploring the Derivative of Exponential Functions

    Subsection 2.4.1 Why do I need to know this?

    Exponential functions are everywhere, as they represent anything that grows in proportion to the present amount. They are also pivotal in Differential equations which are really what describe reality as we know it physically.

    Subsection 2.4.2 What will we learn?

    By the end of this lesson, you will be able to confidently calculate the derivative of exponential functions of the form \(f(x) = a^x\) and \(f(x)=e^x\text{,}\) where \(a\) is a constant positive number. You will gain a deep understanding of how the derivative describes the behavior of these functions.

    Subsection 2.4.3 What will I need to recall in order to learn?

    Before we dive into exponential function derivatives, recall how the derivative represents the rate of change and the slope of a function at a given point. You may wish to review the limit definition of the derivative.

    Subsection 2.4.4 The Main Idea

    In this section, we'll explore the properties and behavior of exponential functions. We'll discuss how exponential functions grow or decay exponentially and examine their unique characteristics. We'll also introduce the concept of the derivative and its connection to the rate of change.

    Subsection 2.4.5 Worked Example

    Find the derivative of \(f(\theta) = e^{\tan(\theta)}\)
    To find the derivative of \(f(\theta) = e^{\tan(\theta)}\text{,}\) we will use the chain rule.
    Let's start by noticing the inner function, we can call it \(u = \tan(\theta)\text{.}\) Now, our function looks like \(f(\theta) = e^u\text{.}\)
    Next, we need to find the derivative of \(f(\theta)\) with respect to \(\theta\text{.}\) Using the chain rule, we have:
    \(\frac{d}{d\theta} f(\theta) = \frac{d}{d\theta} e^u \cdot \frac{du}{d\theta}\)
    The derivative of \(e^u\) with respect to \(u\) is simply \(e^u\text{.}\) This is why calc students do, and should, love \(e^u\) as a function. Now, let's find \(\frac{du}{d\theta}\text{.}\)
    Differentiating \(\tan(\theta)\) with respect to \(\theta\) gives us:
    \(\frac{du}{d\theta} = \sec^2(\theta)\)
    Finally, we can substitute the derivative of \(e^u\) and \(\frac{du}{d\theta}\) back into the chain rule expression:
    \(\frac{d}{d\theta} f(\theta) = e^u \cdot \sec^2(\theta)\)
    Since \(u = \tan(\theta)\text{,}\) we can rewrite the expression as:
    \(\frac{d}{d\theta} f(\theta) = e^{\tan(\theta)} \cdot \sec^2(\theta)\)
    **Sometimes you will see me make a substitution for \(u\) and use the chain rule. Sometimes we will skip this step. It is important to just remember the concept -- if there is an inner function, we need to remember to "respect" the chain rule.
