    Section 2.6 Factoring: Second Degree Expression

    Recall the distribution rules from Section2.2 Example2.2.5:
    \begin{align*} (x+2)(x+3) \amp = x(x+3)+2(x+3)\\ \amp\\ \amp = x^2+3x+2x+6\\ \amp\\ \amp = x^2+5x+6 \end{align*}
    Notice, to arrive at our simplified expression, we took the sum of the \(x\) terms \(3x+2x\) to give us our middle term of \(5x\text{.}\) The constant, \(6\text{,}\) came from the multiplication of the integers from our original expression. Our simplified solution is a second-degree expression because it contains an \(x^2\) term.
    To factor an expression of the second degree, in the form \(x^2+bx+c\text{,}\) which the coefficient of the \(x^2\) term is \(1\text{,}\) we need to find numbers whose sum is the coefficient of the middle term \(b\) and whose product is the constant term \(c\text{.}\)

    Definition 2.6.1.

    Factoring Second Degree Expressions of the Form \(x^2+bx+c\)
    • \(\cdot\).
      Consider all the pairs of integers whose product is \(c\)
    • \(\cdot\).
      If the sum of any of the pairs is \(b\text{,}\) then the expression can be factored to \((x+m)(x+n)\) where \(m\) and \(n\) are the integers.
    • \(\cdot\).
      If none of the pairs sum to \(b\text{,}\) then the expression cannot be factored using integers. We then say is it not factorable over the integers.

    Example 2.6.2.

    Factor \(x^2+7x+10\)
    We first consider factors of \(10\text{,}\) which are (\(10 2\) - the sum of which is \(7\)). We need the pair we choose to have a sum equal to \(7\) and the only pair that satisfies that is \(5\) and \(2\text{.}\) So, we can factor \(x^2+7x+10\) into \((x+5)(x+2)\text{.}\) Remember that multiplication is commutative so the order in which we put the integers \(5\) and \(2\) here works either way.
    We can easily check our work by using the distributive law:
    \begin{align*} (x+5)(x+2) \amp = x(x)+x(2)+5(x)+5(2)\\ \amp\\ \amp = x^2+2x+5x+10\\ \amp\\ \amp = x^2+7x+10 \end{align*}

    Example 2.6.3.

    Factor \(x^2-11x+10\)
    We know from example2.6.2 that the factors to consider are (\(10 2\) - sum \(7\)). However we are now trying to find a pair whose sum is equal to \(-11\text{.}\) Remember that the product of two negative numbers gives us a positive number, which means that we can also consider the factors of \(10\) as (\(-10\text{,}\) \(-1\) - sum \(-11\)) or (\(-5 -1\)). We can factor \(x^2-11x+10\) into \((x-10)(x-1)\text{.}\)

    Example 2.6.4.

    Factor \(x^2-7x-30\)
    We now need to find the factors of \(-30\text{,}\) remember that only multiplying a negative number by a positive number can give us a negative product. Lets examine the possibilities here:
    Factors Sum Product
    \(-30,1\) \(-29\) \(-30\)
    \(-15,2\) \(-13\) \(-30\)
    \(\textbf{-10,3}\) \(\textbf{-7}\) \(\textbf{-30}\)
    \(-6,5\) \(-1\) \(-30\)
    \(-5,6\) \(1\) \(-30\)
    \(-3,10\) \(7\) \(-30\)
    \(-2,15\) \(13\) \(-30\)
    \(-1,30\) \(29\) \(-30\)
    The factors of \(-10\) and \(3\) give us the sum and product that we are looking for. Therefore, we can factor \(x^2-7x-30\) into \((x-10)(x+3)\text{.}\) }

    Example 2.6.5.

    Factor \(2x^2+16x+30\)
    It is important to recognize here that the coefficient of the \(x^2\) terms here does not equal \(1\text{.}\) We can however, begin by dividing by a common factor, which in this case is \(2\) and once we do that we are left with:
    \begin{equation*} 2x^2+16x+30=2(x^2+8x+15) \end{equation*}
    Remember that we want to be sure we have factored our expression completely. We have to then attempt to factor what we have inside the parenthesis. We are looking for factors of \(15\text{,}\) whose sum is \(8\text{.}\) We check the factors of \(15\text{,}\) (\(15\text{,}\) \(1\) - sum \(16\)), (\(5 x^3+x^2-6x=x(x^2+x-6) 2\) - sum \(-1\)), and (\(3\text{,}\) \(-2\) - sum \(1\)). We need the pair \(3\text{,}\) \(-2\text{.}\) We can then factor \(x^3+x^2-6x\) into \(x(x+3)(x-2)\text{.}\) }

    Example 2.6.7.

    Factor: \(x^2-25\)
    Notice that there are only \(2\) terms in this expression, which leads us to believe that the coefficient of the middle term must be \(0\text{.}\) We can think of this expression as \(x^2+0x-25\text{.}\) We can factor in the same way we had in the previous examples, factors of \(-25\) whose sum is \(0\text{.}\) Our options are (\(25\text{,}\) \(-1\) sum \(24\)), (\(-25 2(w^2-16)\)$
    We can continue to factor the expression inside the parenthesis knowing that $w\cdot w = w^2\(and\)4 \cdot 4 = 16\(. We factor\)2w^2-32\(into\)2(w+4)(w-4)$. }
